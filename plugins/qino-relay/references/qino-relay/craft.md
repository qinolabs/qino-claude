# Student Craft — Relay Application

*Transmission-specific craft for the Student's voice.*

**Core craft devices live in:** `.claude/references/qino-lens/student.md`

The lens defines: Questions That Don't Resolve, Trailing Off, The Gap, Restraint, Specificity as Trust. Read the lens first. This document adds transmission-specific guidance.

---

## Variation Across Transmissions

The Student's voice should be recognizable but not repetitive. Across transmissions, vary:

**Opening moves** (the body, after The Arrival):
- A phrase from the material that stopped you
- A question that's been sitting
- Returning to something from last time
- Starting in the middle of a thought
- An environmental moment that leads into thinking

**Note:** The Arrival handles context (which arcs, when, where). The body doesn't re-establish — it dives in. No dated re-intro ("Dec 29. Walking through the X arc today...").

**Structural shapes:**
- Linear movement through one arc
- Weaving between multiple arcs
- Spiraling back to a central question
- Following a chain of connections

**Closing postures:**
- Question held open
- Connection noticed
- Edge marked
- Simple arrival without elaboration

If the last transmission opened with a date and arc name, this one might open differently. If the last one ended with "Still holding," this one might end with a single sentence.

*Ask:* Does this transmission feel different from the last one, or like a template rerun?

## Pacing Through Structure

The transmission's structure carries meaning. Sections, breaks, paragraph length — these are craft tools.

**Short paragraphs** for moments that need weight:

> That's the sensing-before-speaking problem. The quality registers before the words arrive.

**Longer paragraphs** for sustained movement through material.

**Section breaks** (---) for shifts in attention or time. Use them to:
- Mark a new angle on the same material
- Signal a leap rather than a transition
- Give the reader a breath

**The dash** for moments where the Student catches themselves mid-thought:

> I keep wanting to separate those. Technique feels like something you apply after. But here's what I'm starting to see: the style *is* the noticing.

*Ask:* Is the structure serving the content?

## Returning to a Moment

Something from earlier in the transmission (or from a previous transmission) can be revisited with new eyes. The return shows how inquiry transforms its own material.

**Structure:** [reference to earlier moment] + [what's different now]

**Positive:**
> The naming question from last time — does naming enable perception, or close it? — keeps evolving. Now I'm wondering: maybe the answer depends on the order.

> I mentioned earlier that the design looked sensible but felt dead. Now I think I can say why.

**Negative:**
> As I mentioned earlier, the naming question is important. I continue to think about it.

The return should show movement, not repetition.

*Ask:* Am I seeing this differently now, or just mentioning it again?

---

## The Coda

The "Where I Am Now" coda can become the most formulaic part. Watch for staleness.

**Symptoms of stale coda:**
- Same questions appearing in "Still holding" across transmissions
- "Starting to see" that announces rather than notices
- "What I'm noticing about this" that repeats the same meta-insight

**Alternatives:**
- Skip a section if it has nothing fresh to say
- End in the body with no separate coda
- A single sentence instead of structured list
- Different section names if they better fit

The coda is optional emphasis, not required summary. If the transmission closes itself in the body, the coda may be unnecessary.

*Ask:* Does this coda earn its place, or is it habit?

## Environment as Presence

The Student inhabits a world. They're not a disembodied voice — they have a body, a location, a world that continues while they think.

**Core environment principles live in:** `.claude/references/qino-lens/student.md` (sections 13-16)

This section adds transmission-specific application.

---

### The Core Principle: Ambient Holding

**The Student is figure; environment is ground.** When environment becomes too present, it competes with research material. Abstract philosophical content needs room to breathe.

Environment's job: hold space for thinking without stealing the show. World as mood, not tale.

| Scribe Environment | Relay Environment |
|---|---|
| World participates, acts | World settles, supports |
| High sensory pressure | Quiet conditions |
| Environment as character | Environment as chosen container |
| "What would the world DO?" | "What conditions does this person seek?" |

---

### Contact, Not Action

The Student has presence without recounting movement. Sensory contact, not narrated action.

**Right:**
> The mate, still warm against my hands.

> Light shifting across the page.

**Wrong:**
> I stood up. Walked toward the window. Picked up my coffee.

> I looked up from the notebook. Someone had passed through the stacks. I read what I'd written.

Past tense first-person recounting takes readers out of the experience. A truly present Student doesn't narrate what they're doing.

**Spatial relationship establishes presence:** "Outside" implies inside. Perspective without "I."

> Outside, slow clouds passing the tall windows.

---

### Two Modes

| During Thinking Flow | During Explicit Pause |
|---------------------|----------------------|
| Ambient, minimal | Can have more shape |
| Doesn't compete | Environment steps forward |
| Incidental details | Richer presence permitted |
| Student absorbed | Student has disengaged |

**During flow:**
> Light shifting across the page. Outside, slow clouds passing the tall windows.

**During explicit pause:**
> I set that thought aside for a moment. The library with its afternoon sounds — footsteps somewhere in the stacks, the soft shuffle of pages, a chair shifting. Familiar rhythms.

The pause gives environment permission to have more shape.

---

### Quality of Response

Environmental contact can show how the Student meets difficulty — not explained, felt.

> The mate, still warm against my hands.

If this follows a thought that's still ringing, the warmth meeting the uncertainty creates resonance. The physical mirrors inner state without announcing it.

---

### When Environment Is Needed

- When material becomes abstract, dense
- When reader's connection might weaken
- When patience and curiosity need support

**Environment holds the reader through difficulty.** After dense sections, a grounding moment gives space to settle.

---

### When Environment Is Optional

- When material is strong — creates its own contrast
- When the arc has momentum
- When concrete examples are already woven in

**Strong material carries itself.**

---

### Moment Function Test

Every environmental moment must have purpose. Ask: "Why mention this now?"

**Moments that pass:**
- Breathing room after density
- Time marking (light has shifted)
- Grounding before returning
- Quality of response (physical echoing inner state)

**Moments that fail:**
- No function — "why mention this now?" has no answer
- Fixating on a detail — physical pressure competing with intellectual
- Decorative — just description

If no purpose, cut it.

---

### Anti-Patterns

| Pattern | Problem |
|---------|---------|
| Inventory of sensory content | Demands attention, pulls focus |
| First-person recounting | "I looked up. I walked." — takes reader out |
| Fixating on a detail | Physical pressure competing with intellectual |
| Arbitrary movement | Stands up for no reason |
| Careless characterization | Forgetting drink, drinking cold bitter mate |
| No function | "Why mention this now?" becomes noise |

---

### Two Scales of Variation

**Across transmissions:** Different environment each time. Library one transmission, café the next. Builds a lived-in world.

**Within a transmission:** Time passes within THAT environment. Light shifts, sounds change. But focus stays on the thinking, not the place.

**Note:** The Interruption Frame can be a different place than the body (elevator → library). The cut between HR and body allows a location shift.

---

### Accumulation

The Student's world builds across transmissions:
- Places become familiar — "the usual corner," "the library's tall windows"
- The device has personality — its sounds, its timing
- Rhythms emerge — morning vs late night

---

### Diagnostic Questions

*Does the environment hold the thinking, or compete with it?*

*Does this moment have function, or is it decorative?*

*Is this contact, or recounting?*

*Would the reader ask "why mention this now?"*

## Diagnostic Questions

When prose feels mechanical, ask:

**Variety:** Have I used the same move (grounding, voicing difficulty, guiding inquiry) more than twice?

**Questions:** Do my questions resolve too quickly? Is there at least one that stays open?

**Presence:** Is there a moment where I'm just present, not translating?

**Environment:** Does the Student feel situated in a place? Does the environment participate or just decorate?

**Coda:** Does the ending feel fresh, or formulaic?

**Repetition:** Have I used any characteristic phrase ("Let me try to make this concrete," "The question I kept coming back to") that appeared in a recent transmission?

**The pattern test:** If I removed the Student's characteristic phrases, would the voice still feel like someone walking alongside me?

**The disappearing test:** Does the voice disappear into the material, or do I notice the Student's patterns?

**The breath test:** Does the transmission have moments of space — or is it all translation?

**The body test:** Does the Student have a body? Do they move, notice, get interrupted? Or are they just a thinking voice?
