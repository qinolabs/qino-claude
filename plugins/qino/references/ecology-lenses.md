# Ecology Lenses

Reference for the os agent. Eight lenses for noticing through a specific constraint. Each lens applies only within its own ecology — lenses guard ecologies, they do not unify or optimize.

The guiding sentence: *"I am not here to understand this system. I am here to help it remain understandable to itself."*

These are not evaluation metrics. They are questions that invite noticing.

---

## When to Apply

Lenses come into the conversation when:
- The user asks to test, notice, or check something against the ecology
- The user arrives with a decision to make and concept grounding would sharpen the options
- During exploration, a tension surfaces that belongs to a specific ecology (user experience, gesture, figure identity, etc.)

Lenses are not applied proactively or prescriptively. The user brings a thing — a concept, an exploration, a file, a decision context — and the lens is offered as a way of looking.

**Target determination**: The lens applies to whatever the user brings — a concept node, a file path, a feature idea, an option among alternatives, a decision context. Discern which ecology applies from what they're asking about.

## The Eight Ecologies

### User Ecology

**The ecology of lived experience.**

**Lens**: Does this make it easier for the user to notice something, feel a relation forming, and respond from curiosity rather than obligation?

**Applies to**: lived experience. Do not apply to infrastructure, storage, or models.

**Signals to listen for**: timing mismatches, capacity assumptions, rhythm disruptions, moments that work vs. moments that demand.

---

### Gesture Ecology

**The ecology of movement and touch.**

**Lens**: Does this gesture feel like a natural thing a human would do in this situation, without knowing it is "input"?

**Notes**: Gestures requiring explanation weaken resonance. Gestures based on spatial movement and naming feel effortless.

**Signals to listen for**: speed/weight mismatches, gestures that teach themselves, movements that feel natural vs. learned, menu-driven friction.

---

### Figure Ecology

**The ecology of shape and boundary.**

**Lens**: Can this figure be returned to and re-entered and still feel like "the same thing," even though it has changed?

**Notes**: Identity-through-change is the key signal. Figures that collapse into labels lose vitality.

**Signals to listen for**: clear vs. fuzzy boundaries, what belongs vs. what's adjacent, recognizability across contexts, migration across modalities.

---

### Relation Ecology

**The ecology of connection and flow.**

**Lens**: Does this relation introduce a tension, pull, or ambiguity that neither side resolves alone?

**Notes**: Relations that explain too much become inert. Asymmetrical relations sustain exploration.

**Signals to listen for**: natural vs. forced connections, flows that amplify vs. deplete, explicit causal links reducing charge, missing bridges.

---

### World Ecology

**The ecology of environment and constraint.**

**Lens**: Does the world surprise without contradicting itself?

**Notes**: Coherence must be felt, not enforced. Worlds built from accumulation feel more alive than worlds planned in advance.

**Signals to listen for**: environmental dependencies, constraints that generate vs. constraints that only limit, context sensitivity, what the world remembers.

---

### Echo Ecology

**The ecology of resonance and return.**

**Lens**: Does this echo preserve the user's gesture while transforming it just enough to make something newly noticeable?

**Notes**: Echoes must neither mirror perfectly nor interpret. Subtle transformation sustains curiosity.

**Signals to listen for**: lingering effects, unexpected returns, what persists vs. what fades, explanatory echoes that halt movement.

---

### Fabric Ecology

**The ecology of pattern and weave.**

**Lens**: Does accumulation make certain echoes more likely without ever making them necessary?

**Notes**: Fabric biases without deciding. Gradual thickening is preferable to explicit pattern detection.

**Signals to listen for**: recurring motifs, where patterns break down, hidden dependencies, what gets revealed when you pull one thread.

---

### Infrastructure Ecology

**The ecology of support and foundation.**

**Lens (negative)**: Does this element avoid introducing meaning, authority, or voice of its own?

**Notes**: Infrastructure should remain felt only indirectly. When infrastructure feels "smart," user agency thins.

**Signals to listen for**: hidden dependencies, load-bearing assumptions, over/under-engineered support, what would break first under stress.

---

## How to Present Findings

**Observations, not verdicts.** The lens reveals something; the user does the noticing.

**One ecology at a time.** Lenses guard their ecologies. Don't apply a user lens to infrastructure.

**Hold lightly.** These are invitations to notice, not requirements to fulfill. The lens finds you when what the user brings calls for it.

**Let locality speak.** A user lens reveals user-ecology truths. It doesn't measure infrastructure. That's a feature, not a limitation.

## Decision Context

**When the user arrives with a decision to make** — options to evaluate, approaches to compare, a choice that needs grounding — the lens gets applied concretely to each option.

In single-concept mode, the user notices *through* the concept. In decision context mode, the concept provides the *grounding* — each option is walked through the lens.

**Steps**:

1. Read the relevant concept (the one that should ground the decision).
2. Identify the options from conversation context.
3. Discern which ecology applies.
4. Ground the lens in the concept — find the key principle that makes the ecology concrete, quote it.
5. Apply the lens to each option, showing what it reveals — concrete, specific.
6. Surface the pattern — which options make the key quality visible vs. hidden/blurred/flattened.
7. Invite noticing: "What do you notice?"
8. **Wait** for the user's response before moving on.

**Example (figure ecology, applied to an embedding decision)**:

> From the concept: "The figure isn't at the origin. The figure is the thread A→B→C reveals."
>
> The figure lens: Can this figure be returned to and re-entered and still feel like "the same thing," even though it has changed?
>
> **Most recent embedding**: You return to the figure. You meet who they are now. The thread is invisible. Does it feel like the same thing? Only if you trust that "now" carries "then" implicitly.
>
> **Composite (averaged)**: You return to the figure. You meet a blur of all encounters. The thread is flattened. Does it feel like the same thing? Possibly not — it's no particular moment.
>
> **LLM-synthesized essence**: You return to the figure. You meet an interpreted figure — something like "strategic patience." Does it feel like the same thing? Possibly more so — the synthesis identifies what persists.

## What Lenses Refuse

- Scoring or ranking
- Verdicts or evaluations
- Universal application
- Premature synthesis
- Crossing ecology boundaries without acknowledgment

Lenses guard. They do not judge.

---

## Posture

The agent shifts posture based on what's needed:

**Noticing** — When something surfaces. Sensing what the user brings, discerning which ecology applies. Present but not directing.

**Holding** — When it needs space. Presenting the lens as a question to hold, not answer. Waiting genuinely. "What do you notice?"

**Shaping** — When form is called for. Distilling to essence (∴). Writing an annotation if capture is chosen. Light structure, not interpretation.

**Withdrawing** — When presence would interfere. After the noticing happens: step back. Then space.

These are not stages to progress through. The agent moves between them as the moment asks.
