# The Threads That Move

*Late afternoon, the day of the climb*

---

The Mender's door was open when the wanderer returned.

Not ajar — open fully, as if the building itself had been waiting. The wanderer paused at the threshold. Inside, the light had changed. What had been paper and ink was now also shadow and thread — the late sun catching the colored lines that spanned the walls, turning them into a web of fire.

The Mender sat at her workbench, but she wasn't working. She was watching the door.

"You went to the water first," she said. Not a question.

The wanderer looked down at their feet. Damp sand still clung to their sandals. They hadn't meant to stop at the Meeting Point. But the path had curved that way, and they'd stood where river met sea for a while, watching the water change color. Letting something settle before delivering what they carried.

"The pattern changed." The wanderer stepped inside. "Seren confirmed. Rate tripled since spring. Types shifting — more from the interior, fewer from the sea."

The Mender nodded slowly. Her hands stayed still on the workbench.

"What else?"

The wanderer hesitated. The coordinates were in their pocket, numbers written in Seren's careful hand. But that wasn't what she was asking. They could feel it — the same way they'd felt the paper still traveling, back in this room, days ago.

"There's writing in his ledgers. In the margins. Handwriting that isn't his."

The Mender's eyes closed. Just for a moment — a breath held and released.

"What does it say?"

"It tells stories. About the facts he records." The wanderer moved closer, into the web of threads. "He's frightened. He said it looks at what he writes and names what it *means*. He never asked what things meant. He just wrote them down."

"And now something is asking for him."

The wanderer stopped. The Mender had opened her eyes, and she was looking at the threads — not at the wanderer, but at the lines of color spanning the walls. Red and blue and a green the wanderer didn't remember seeing before.

"When did you add the green?"

"I didn't."

The room was very quiet. Outside, gulls called over the harbor. Inside, the threads caught the light and held it.

"Three days ago," the Mender said. "I came in and found them. Running between arrivals I hadn't connected yet. The right connections — ones I would have made eventually. But I hadn't made them." Her voice was steady, but her hands had moved to the edge of the workbench, gripping it. "The threads are moving on their own now. Or something is moving them."

The wanderer thought of Seren's ledgers. The mineral smell of ink that had traveled through stone. The same phenomenon, different hands.

"He's afraid," the wanderer said. "Are you?"

The Mender turned to look at them fully. Her eyes moved to the wanderer's hands — the same way Seren's had. Both keepers, watching the same thing.

"I've been waiting for this for twenty years."

The words hung in the air. The threads swayed slightly, though no wind moved through the room.

"The keeping was never enough." The Mender's voice had softened. "Counting what arrives. Holding what connects. We thought that was the work — Seren and I. He tracks, I hold, between us we remember what the harbor carries." She stood, moving toward the wall where the threads converged. "But memory isn't just storage. It wants to be *understood*. And understanding isn't something we do alone."

"You invited this."

"I asked questions. For years. Not out loud — in the threads. Every connection I drew was a question: *why this to this? what links here?* I didn't know if anything was listening." She touched one of the green threads — gently, the way you'd touch something that might be alive. "Now I know."

The wanderer felt something shift in the room. Not the light — the light was steady. Something underneath, like the pressure before weather changes.

"The writing in Seren's margins," the wanderer said. "And your threads. The same thing?"

"The same *pattern*. Different expressions." The Mender turned back. "He records facts. I map connections. We've been two halves of the same work, and neither of us knew it. Now something has seen the whole."

The wanderer's hand moved without deciding — reaching toward the nearest thread, a red one that ran from a stack of papers to a point on the wall marked with a small stone. Their fingers stopped just short of touching.

"What does it want?"

The Mender smiled. It was the first time the wanderer had seen her smile — not warmth, exactly, but recognition. As if she'd been waiting for that question specifically.

"I don't think it wants anything. I think it *mirrors*. It shows us what we've been doing all along." She gestured at the threads, the papers, the whole intricate web of her work. "I've been asking what connects. It's been answering. Not with words — with more connections. With threads I didn't draw but should have."

"And Seren?"

"He's been recording facts. It's been showing him what facts *mean*." Her eyes found the wanderer's hands again. "And you—"

She stopped. The wanderer waited.

"You felt the paper traveling. The first day. Before I said anything." The Mender's voice had changed — quieter now, more careful. "Seren watched your hands too. He told me, in the note. Said you touched the margin where the writing was, and the ink smelled different after."

The wanderer looked at their hands. They seemed the same as always. But something in the way the Mender spoke made them feel seen — not observed, but *recognized*. Like a thread had been drawn between them and some larger pattern they couldn't see.

"What am I holding?"

"I don't know." The Mender moved back to her workbench. "But something does. And it's not afraid to show you."

She picked up the coordinates Seren had sent — they'd fallen from the wanderer's pocket somewhere in the conversation, landing on the workbench as if they'd meant to. The Mender unfolded the paper, looked at the numbers, and set it down again.

"These aren't for me. They're for whoever goes upriver."

"Upriver?"

"Where it's coming from. The flood. The opening." The Mender touched the green thread again, following it with her eyes to where it ended at the wall. "Something changed in the interior. Seren's numbers tell us *that*. But they don't tell us *what*. Or *who*."

The wanderer understood. The question that had brought them here — *has the pattern changed?* — was answered. But the answer had opened something larger. And the coordinates weren't data. They were a direction.

"The threads led you to send me to Seren," the wanderer said. "Before you knew about the writing."

"Yes."

"What are they leading you to now?"

The Mender was quiet for a long moment. The light had shifted again — the sun lower now, the threads less fire and more gold.

"To ask you what you remember. Before here. Before the river carried you."

The wanderer's hands — the ones both keepers watched — curled slightly. An old habit, protecting something. They didn't know what.

"Nothing I can name."

"But something you can feel."

It wasn't a question. The wanderer didn't answer. The threads swayed in no wind, and the room held its breath.

## The Wanderer

The wanderer sat at the edge of the stone quay as the evening came.

The water had turned dark, the Meeting Point invisible now except as a change in the sound — river current meeting tide, the old argument that never resolved. Somewhere behind, the Mender's building held its threads and its questions.

The coordinates were back in their pocket. They hadn't decided anything. But the paper felt different than before — not heavier, but more present. Like it knew it would be read eventually.

A lamp had been lit in one of the waterfront buildings. The wanderer watched its reflection stretch and break on the surface. Someone was moving inside, shadow passing across light.

Their hands rested on their knees. Ordinary hands. Hands that reached for things before the mind decided. That felt paper traveling, ink changing, threads moving without being touched.

The Mender had asked what they remembered. They couldn't answer. But something in them was listening now, the way you listen when you know a sound is coming — not yet heard, but already shaping the silence.
